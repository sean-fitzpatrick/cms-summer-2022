Why OER?

Cost

This is the most visible aspect of OER: it tends to be free.

Cost is not unimportant: many students struggle to afford course materials.

But it's not all (or we wouldn't be here)

Flexibility

Instructors have freedom to edit, adapt, and combine OER.

  • Add a chapter to a book that almost fits

  • Canadianize the spelling!

  • Reorganize the contents

  • Fix typos

  • Change a definition

Access

Remember Fall 2020?

Did your students get their books on time?

  • Mine did. Early, in fact.

  • OER tends to be online. Why not?

  • Licensing allows students to print as needed

  • There are no access codes or crappy online viewers

Quality

OER is free, but it's not very good, right?

(At least, according to the publisher reps)

This was true 10 years ago. It's not true any more.

Where OER still falls short, it is due to lack of people-hours to work on content.

PreTeXt

PreTeXt

PreTeXt is an XML vocabulary and toolchain for authoring open textbooks.

From common source, PreTeXt can generate HTML, PDF, EPUB, Jupyter, and Braille.

PreTeXt HTML boasts features such as interactive graphics and exercises, live code cells, video, and a portable interface that works well on mobile devices.

You can also use it to make slides!

Runestone

Runestone Academy is a website that provides free, interactive textbooks, mostly in computer science.

The next step for PreTeXt is integration with Runestone.

A PreTeXt book running on Runestone will allow students to log in, and complete homework, take notes, answer reading questions, etc.

NSF-funded projects

UTMOST, v1

UTMOST stands for Undergraduate Teaching in Mathematics with Open Software and Textbooks.

First NSF grant funded development of SageMathCell and CoCalc, and the Open Textbook Initiative.

Subsequent NSF grants have funded PreTeXt, and total several million dollars.

UTMOST, v2

Most recent grant used to fund PreTeXt development, including several AIM workshops.

One major advancement has been the development of Braille output.

Previous work used analytics (built into the book) to study how students used their textbooks.

Research by Vilma Mesa (University of Michigan) generated heat maps to show how students used their books.

TBIL and CheckIt

Steven Clontz has written a problem-generation system called CheckIt, designed to generate assessments aligned to standards.

This was a key tool for an NSF-funded project on Team-Based Inquiry Learning.

This summer they enter their second year of training instructors and generating content.

Adventures in Alternative Assessment

A review of non-expert attempts to liberate my gradebook

Sean Fitzpatrick
University of Lethbridge

June 5th, 2022

Initial baby-steps: tutorials

The passive tutorial

  • Intended primarily as homework help

  • Students ask questions, instructor solves them

  • Mostly benefits those doing well already

  • Attendance optional (and low)

The active tutorial

  • Students receive a worksheet

  • Group work is encouraged

  • Instructor provides assistance to students

  • “Lecturing” limited to introduction or common difficulty

  • Work graded for feedback

  • Resubmission permitted

Standards-Based Grading, part 1

The catalysts

  1. A “Curated Courses” workshop (PreTeXt-based) in Austin, TX

    Steven Clontz presented on using SBG

  2. The Callysto project

    I was the Lethbridge site director for Phase 1

    This was supposed to come with a teaching buyout; I got TA money instead

SBG for Calculus 1

I took the plunge for Math 1560.

This was not 100% SBG. “In-class exercises” were 30% of the grade.

I kept tests, and online homework.

The good

  • Crowdmark submission and a good TA meant students got feedback within 24 hours

  • The (points-based) grades went up

  • Biggest change was the final: average and median both +12%

  • More students earned A grades, despite higher cut-offs

The bad

  • I spent way to much time explaining our system

  • Neither Crowdmark nor our LMS were really set up to handle resubmission

  • Some students kept trying to turn it into homework

  • The percentage of students failing did not improve

And the ugly: did I mention this was a class of 240?

Even with support, this was way too much work.

Strictly cumulative grading

My introduction

In Fall 2018, our Teaching Centre sent a group (including me!) to the MRU iSoTL Symposium in Banff.

One of the talks was by Katrin Becker (MRU) on “strictly cumulative grading”.

This is a system where:

  • Students start the year at zero

  • Everything earns points

  • All that matters is the total, not where the points come from

My implementation

I decided to try this for Math 2090, a course intended primarily for future elementary school teachers.

First time was a very last-minute assignment. I appreciated the flexibility this system allowed me.

Course included:

  • Quizzes
  • Assignments
  • Presentations
  • Forum Discussion
  • A Curriculum project
  • A Final Exam

The Good

  • Fantastic for encouraging discussion, especially on forums

  • Encouraged collaboration and creativity

  • Allowed us to have fun

  • Most students did very well

  • Students had agency and flexibility

The Bad

  • Not that much, to be honest

  • High-performing students were able to finish early, and stopped attending

  • Unsure how to translate to “traditional” math classes

Standards-based grading, part 2

A small class, at last

Last fall, I brought “grading for growth” to Math 2575. There were fewer than 10 students in the class!

Students were asked for input on course design. They wanted:

  • Online homework

  • A project

  • No tests (once they learned that was an option)

Majority of grade determined by standards-aligned assignments, with unlimited revision.

Results

Success! But this was a small group of strong students.

Revisions let us focus on writing quality as well.

It also meant challenging problems weren't an issue.

Question: how to balance individual standards assessment with the desire for collaboration

Where am I at now?

Why am I not ungrading?

I know better, so why do I still use points?

  1. Class size: often 100+

  2. Grading resources: disappearing

  3. Institutional inertia:

    • Coordination across sections

    • Student expectations

    • Sadly, lowering pressure can backfire

How do I make up for it?

Can I keep points-based grading in large classes (for logistical reasons) but make it less unfair and more focused on learning?

Here's what I used last semester.

  1. Assignments: time during class, groups encouraged

  2. Tests: two-stage, when possible

  3. Final exam: why?

  4. Revisions: only once, but on almost everything

  5. Extensions: almost always

Questions (from me, for you!)

  • Can we ungrade efficiently? (Think: 1 instructor, 200 students, no TAs)

  • Is it fair to grade for completion? (e.g. exam wrappers)

  • In any system, how do we mitigate the “cutoff dilemma”?
